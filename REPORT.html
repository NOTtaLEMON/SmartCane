1Introduction

Visual impairment is a global public-health challenge: the World Health Organization estimates that over 2.2 billion people live with some form of vision loss, of whom 43 million are completely blind. In India, the National Programme for Control of Blindness reports nearly 5 million blind citizens and 70 million with significant visual impairment — numbers that are disproportionately concentrated in lower-income groups for whom commercial assistive technology remains entirely out of reach. The traditional white cane, while culturally accepted and affordable, communicates only what is within an arm's length of tactile sweep; it cannot detect overhead obstacles, distinguish a kerb from a step, sense vehicles, or summon help when its user falls.

The Smart Cane Clip-On project takes the white cane that visually-impaired users already carry and adds, in the form of a compact clip-on module, three things it currently lacks: multi-sensor obstacle awareness, computer-vision-based scene understanding, and automatic emergency response. The crucial design choice is that none of these features require new compute hardware on the cane itself; instead, the system leverages the user's existing Android smartphone for inference, geolocation, and SMS dispatch, keeping the cane lightweight and the parts cost under ₹2,000.

This Experiential Learning report documents the design, implementation, and bench-validation of the prototype. The work spans embedded firmware in C++/Arduino on an ESP32, an Android application in Kotlin with CameraX and TensorFlow Lite, and a Python-Streamlit dashboard for developer-side telemetry visualisation. Together, the three subsystems form a complete, end-to-end pipeline from sensor read to spoken alert.

2Problem Definition

2.1 Problem Statement

Statement. Existing assistive devices for visually-impaired pedestrians fall into two unsatisfactory categories: (i) traditional white canes that provide only tactile sensing within arm's reach and cannot detect drops, overhead obstacles, or sudden falls; and (ii) commercial smart canes that cost between US\$300 and US\$800, putting them out of reach for the majority of users in India and similar low-income markets. There is no affordable, locally manufacturable solution that simultaneously delivers multi-modal obstacle detection, on-device computer vision, and autonomous fall-response — despite the fact that the user typically already owns a smartphone capable of running such workloads.

3Objectives

3.1 Primary Objectives

  1. Build a multi-sensor clip-on module incorporating LiDAR, ToF, and IMU sensors on an ESP32 platform, with all components under ₹2,000.
  2. Develop an Android application that consumes the cane's telemetry over Serial/WiFi and performs real-time YOLOv8 object detection on the rear camera feed.
  3. Implement autonomous fall response that correlates cane-IMU and phone-IMU fall signatures and dispatches GPS-tagged SMS alerts to up to five emergency contacts without user intervention.
  4. Achieve ≤33 ms inference latency on mid-range Android hardware to maintain a real-time obstacle-warning experience.

3.2 Secondary Objectives

  1. Build a Python-Streamlit developer dashboard supporting Serial, WiFi, and Mock connection modes for hardware-free UI work.
  2. Train and deploy seven YOLOv8n TFLite models that run in parallel: one main COCO general detector, one segmentation head, and five fine-tuned single-class hazard specialists (pothole, electric pole, steps/kerb, tree, car).
  3. Document a reproducible model-training pipeline (Roboflow → Ultralytics YOLO → TFLite → Android assets) so that future cohorts can extend the obstacle taxonomy without re-deriving the workflow.
  4. Verify all-day battery operation (≥8 hours continuous use) on a 3.7 V Li-ion cell through an MT3608 boost converter.

Step-by-step Procedure

  1. Requirement analysis — interviewed two visually-impaired users at a local NGO to identify pain points; documented top six requirements.
  2. Component selection — compared ESP32 against STM32 and RP2040; chose ESP32 for built-in WiFi and Arduino tooling.
  3. Hardware integration — built a perfboard prototype, then transitioned to an ABS-plastic clip-on enclosure (150 × 150 × 70 mm).
  4. Firmware development — implemented a 3-level priority cascade (Fall → LiDAR obstacle → ToF drop) and the CSV packet.
  5. Android development — built the foreground SOS service, then the CameraX + TFLite pipeline.
  6. Model training — validated the pipeline with public datasets first, then collected custom pothole and kerb images.
  7. Bench testing — characterised distance accuracy, fall recall, and inference latency against measurable targets.

5Project Execution

5.1 Planning and Design Stage

The first month was spent on planning and component-level design. Three competing electrical architectures were sketched and evaluated against four criteria: (a) battery life, (b) sensor cost, (c) firmware complexity, and (d) field-serviceability. The winning architecture (ESP32 + commodity ToF sensors + smartphone as compute host) scored highest on all four because it pushed the heaviest cost — computation — onto a device the user already owns.

Sensor placement on the cane enclosure was determined by simple geometry: the TF-Luna LiDAR points forward at the typical sweep angle (≈15° downward from horizontal), the VL53L0X points straight down with a 15° cone, and the MPU6050 is rigidly mounted to the cane shaft so any drop/fall is unambiguously detectable.

5.2 Implementation Stage

Implementation was tracked through a Git-based workflow with five long-lived branches (firmware, android, ml, dashboard, integration) and weekly merges into main. The firmware was the first subsystem to reach completion because it has the smallest external surface area; the Android app followed once a stable wire format was agreed upon.

Key implementation challenges resolved. (i) The Adafruit MPU6050 library introduced ~40 ms of jitter, which was unacceptable for fall detection — replaced with direct I²C register reads. (ii) The GPU delegate occasionally crashed on devices without OpenGL ES 3.1; wrapped in runCatching with a 4-thread CPU fallback. (iii) Android 12+ requires a foreground-service notification or the OS kills the SOS service after ~30 s; added a persistent low-priority notification. (iv) Early TTS announced every detected label, which produced unusable audio flooding when many objects were visible — switched to top-1 detection per frame with a 1.5 s per-label cooldown and a 0.75 confidence threshold (0.60 for pothole). (v) Hazard-detection push notifications were tried and removed: they added no information beyond TTS while burying the user under banner spam. (vi) NMS IOU was raised from 0.45 to 0.60 so that multiple overlapping people in a frame remain separate boxes. (vii) The planned single unified 8-class segmentation model was dropped in favour of per-hazard specialized YOLOv8n detectors, which outperformed the unified head on the small, imbalanced datasets we had access to.

6.2 Techniques Applied

  1. Sensor fusion with priority arbitration — three alert sources (Fall, LiDAR obstacle, ToF drop) evaluated every tick, single-output buzzer driven by highest-priority winner.
  2. Float16 quantisation — halved model size with negligible mAP loss, enabling 320×320 inference under 33 ms.
  3. GPU delegation with graceful CPU fallback — runtime detection of OpenGL ES 3.1 capability.
  4. Validate-pipeline-then-collect-data — built and proved the entire training-to-deployment pipeline on synthetic and public data before annotating custom imagery.
  5. Mock-mode driven UI development — the dashboard's mock packet generator allowed visualisation work to proceed in parallel with firmware bring-up.
  6. Foreground service with persistent notification — standard Android pattern to keep SOS dispatch alive across screen-off events.

7Partial Results

7.1 Initial Findings

The first integrated prototype was running by end of Phase 2. Initial bench tests revealed several issues that drove subsequent refinements:

  • Drift in MPU6050 readings when the Adafruit library was used — sub-sampling at uneven intervals produced ghost falls during normal sweeping. Fix: switched to direct register reads at a fixed 100 Hz interval.
  • VL53L0X spurious 0 mm readings on highly reflective surfaces (polished tile). Fix: median filter over the last 5 samples.
  • YOLOv8 TFLite output tensor shape mismatch between detection (1×84×8400) and segmentation (1×116×8400) models. Fix: auto-detect tensor shape at load time and branch into the appropriate post-processor.
  • SMS dispatch silently failed on Android 13 without SEND_SMS permission. Fix: added a permission-request guard with a clear rationale dialog.

7.2 Iterative Improvements

Each issue above produced an iteration of the firmware or app. The improvements compound: by the end of Phase 4, fall-detection recall had climbed from 81% to 97%, and the false-positive rate during normal walking trials dropped from 11/hour to 0/hour. Inference latency on the reference Snapdragon 695 device improved from 96 ms (initial, CPU-only) to 28 ms (GPU delegate enabled).

8.2 Discussion

The most consequential design decision was pushing all computer vision onto the user's phone. This kept the cane light, the cost low, and the system inherits the phone's network, GPS, and SMS for free. The cost of this decision is that inference quality is bounded by the phone's GPU; in practice, the Snapdragon 695 (a mid-range chip released in 2021) easily meets the latency budget, so the choice is favourable for the foreseeable mid-range Android market.

A second trade-off concerns the CSV wire format. Binary protocols (Protobuf, MessagePack) would be ~3× more efficient on the wire, but at 10 Hz × 4 fields the bandwidth is negligible (under 1 kbps), while the debuggability of plain text was instrumental during integration — every member of the team could open a serial monitor and immediately understand what the cane was reporting.

Limitations. No live trial with visually-impaired users has yet been conducted — results are restricted to bench tests and sighted-user wearing tests. Highly reflective surfaces still occasionally fool the VL53L0X. These limitations are queued for the next development phase.

9Prototype

9.1 Prototype Description

The prototype is housed in an ABS-plastic clip-on enclosure (150 × 150 × 70 mm) that attaches to a standard white cane. The enclosure houses an ESP32 DevKit, the three sensors (TF-Luna, VL53L0X, MPU6050), a 16×2 I²C LCD, an active buzzer, a 3.7 V Li-ion cell, an MT3608 boost converter, and a KD slide switch for master power.

9.2 Development of the Prototype

The prototype went through three physical revisions:

  1. Rev 0 (perfboard breadboard) — used to validate sensor wiring and the firmware loop before any 3D printing.
  2. Rev 1 (perfboard inside ABS box) — first wearable form factor; sensor cutouts had to be re-positioned because the LiDAR aperture was partially blocked by the cane mounting strap.
  3. Rev 2 (final) — refined cutouts, recessed buzzer port for water resistance, and an external KD slide switch for master power.

9.3 Testing and Validation

Testing was performed in three environments:

  • Indoor lab (controlled lighting, smooth floor) — baseline accuracy measurements.
  • Outdoor pavement (mixed surfaces, kerbs, parked vehicles) — real-world obstacle detection.
  • Stairwell (downstairs descent) — drop detection on 15 cm step risers.

Validation criteria were the six metrics tabulated in Table 8.1. Each metric was measured over a minimum of 30 trials. The fall-detection trial was performed by dropping the cane onto a foam mat from a 1.2 m height (cane only, no human).

10Conclusion

10.1 Summary of Findings

The Smart Cane Clip-On project successfully demonstrated that a low-cost (₹1850), modular assistive device can integrate multi-sensor obstacle detection, on-device YOLOv8 object detection, and autonomous SMS-based emergency response — meeting all six committed performance targets. The key insight is that the user's existing smartphone is a free, sufficient compute platform: pushing inference onto the phone eliminates the largest cost driver in comparable commercial products.

The project also produced a reproducible end-to-end ML pipeline (Roboflow → Ultralytics → TFLite → Android assets) and a Streamlit-based dashboard that supports hardware-free development through a mock connection mode. Both artefacts can be re-used by future EL cohorts to extend the obstacle taxonomy without re-deriving the workflow.
