A Low-Cost Clip-On Assistive Device for the Visually Impaired Integrating Multi-Modal Sensing and On-Device Deep Learning

A multi-sensor, smartphone-tethered navigation aid combining LiDAR, ToF, IMU and a YOLOv8 inference pipeline

Anand Raghavan, Aravind R, Rishab H R, Shreya Yadawad, Anushka Sarkar

Department of Civil Engineering, RV College of Engineering, Bengaluru-560059, India

Mentor: Dr. Varuna M, Faculty, Department of Civil Engineering

Experiential Learning Project — Phase II RV College of Engineering 2024-25
Abstract—Visual impairment affects an estimated 285 million people worldwide and existing electronic travel aids (ETAs) remain either prohibitively expensive or limited to single-sense range-finding. This paper presents Smart Cane Clip-On, a modular assistive system that augments a conventional white cane with an ESP32-based multi-sensor module and an Android smartphone running on-device YOLOv8 object detection. The cane fuses TF-Luna LiDAR (forward distance), VL53L0X time-of-flight (downward drop detection), and MPU6050 inertial sensing (fall detection) into a 10 Hz packet stream consumed over USB-serial or WiFi-WebSocket by an Android service. The smartphone runs seven quantised TFLite models in parallel — a main COCO YOLOv8n detector, a YOLOv8n-seg head, and five single-class hazard specialists (pothole, electric pole, steps/kerb, tree, car) — all at 320×320 float16 with optional GPU delegation, producing audio cues through Android TextToSpeech (top-1 detection per frame, 1.5 s cooldown, confidence threshold 0.75; pothole 0.60) and triggering autonomous SMS alerts to up to five emergency contacts upon fall detection. A Streamlit-based Python dashboard provides live telemetry visualisation. The complete bill of materials stays under ₹2,000 (≈US\$24), making the device accessible to low-income users in the developing world. Preliminary bench testing demonstrated obstacle detection up to 8 m, drop detection accuracy above 95% for steps deeper than 15 cm, a pothole-specialist mAP50 of 0.749, and end-to-end multi-model inference latency below 33 ms on mid-range Android hardware.

Index Terms—Assistive technology, visual impairment, electronic travel aid, sensor fusion, YOLOv8, TensorFlow Lite, ESP32, edge inference, smart cane.

I. Introduction

According to the World Health Organization, at least 2.2 billion people globally have some form of vision impairment, of whom over 43 million are blind [1]. In India alone, an estimated 4.95 million people are blind and 70 million are visually impaired [2]. The traditional white cane — cheap, lightweight, and culturally accepted — remains the dominant mobility aid, but it fundamentally provides only tactile information within an arm's length and offers no awareness of obstacles above the cane sweep, drops in elevation, head-height hazards, or contextual cues such as vehicles or signage.

Commercial smart canes such as the WeWalk and SmartCane address parts of this gap but cost between US\$300 and US\$800, which places them out of reach for users in price-sensitive markets. Recent research prototypes have explored ultrasonic-based detection [3], camera-only deep-learning solutions [4], and IoT-enabled emergency response [5]; however, few systems combine all three of (a) multi-sensor obstacle detection, (b) on-device computer vision, and (c) autonomous fall-triggered emergency response in a single, sub-US\$30 form factor.

This work presents an integrated system that addresses these gaps:

• Multi-modal hardware — an ESP32 concentrates LiDAR, ToF and IMU signals into a 10 Hz packet stream.
• Edge AI — the user's existing Android phone runs seven YOLOv8n TFLite models in parallel (one general COCO detector plus five fine-tuned single-class hazard specialists and one segmentation head) with GPU delegation, eliminating cloud dependency.
• Autonomous safety — correlated cane-IMU and phone-side fall signatures trigger SMS alerts with GPS coordinates to up to five contacts.
• Affordability — total parts cost <₹2,000, achieved through commodity sensors and the user-owned smartphone serving as the compute platform.

II. Related Work

The literature on electronic travel aids spans four broad categories: (i) ultrasonic-only obstacle detection, (ii) camera-only deep-learning systems, (iii) hybrid IoT-cloud solutions, and (iv) wearable haptic systems. Six representative recent works are summarised below and contrasted with the system proposed here.

A. Ultrasonic and ToF-Based Canes

Mai et al. [3] proposed an ultrasonic-array smart cane with vibration feedback, achieving detection up to 4 m at the cost of multiple HC-SR04 modules and significant beam-pattern dead zones. Lavanya Narayani et al. [6] used a single ultrasonic sensor coupled with a GPS module for emergency tracking but lacked any object-classification capability and relied on a GSM module rather than the user's existing smartphone.

B. Camera and Deep-Learning Approaches

Gad et al. [4] demonstrated a Raspberry-Pi-based system running MobileNet-SSD for indoor obstacle recognition, achieving 84% mAP but at a unit cost of ≈US\$120 and a battery life under three hours. Nataraj et al. [7] proposed a YOLOv5 pipeline on Jetson Nano, with strong accuracy but an unwearable 0.8 kg payload.

C. IoT and Cloud-Connected Systems

Wohiduzzaman et al. [8] presented a cloud-tethered cane that uploads sensor data to AWS for processing; the architecture works but introduces unbounded latency over poor cellular links and a recurring data-plan cost.

D. Wearable and Haptic Feedback Systems

Skulimowski et al. [9] explored stereo-vision goggles with electrotactile feedback, achieving rich spatial perception at a unit cost above US\$500 and requiring extensive user training.

The system is organised around three loosely coupled subsystems — the cane firmware, the Android application, and the Python development/analytics dashboard — communicating through a single 3-field CSV packet format. This decoupling means each subsystem can be developed, tested, and replaced independently.

A. Wire Format

All sensor data flows in one direction (cane → phone) using the format dist_fwd,dist_drop,fall_flag\n emitted at 10 Hz. The packet is human-readable, robust to occasional byte loss, and trivially parseable on both Kotlin and Python sides.

IV. Methodology

A. Sensor Fusion and Alert Arbitration

Three alert sources (fall, LiDAR obstacle, ToF drop) are evaluated every firmware loop, but the single buzzer can express only one alert pattern at a time. A strict priority cascade resolves contention: a higher-priority alert always preempts a lower-priority one for the buzzer channel, while packet transmission remains unaffected. The priority scheme is given in (1):

P(t) = arg maxi { wi · ci(t) }, i ∈ {Fall, LiDAR, Drop}   (1)

where ci(t) ∈ {0,1} is the truth of condition i at tick t, and wi are integer priority weights with wFall > wLiDAR > wDrop.

B. Fall Detection

Fall detection is implemented as a single-threshold test on the squared magnitude of the accelerometer vector:

F(t) = 1 if  ax2(t) + ay2(t) + az2(t) > θF, else 0   (2)

with θF=24000 (raw register units squared), chosen empirically to fire on free-fall+impact while rejecting normal cane tap-and-sweep motion. A confirmed F(t)=1 triggers a continuous 5 s buzzer alarm and a flag the Android service consumes to dispatch SMS (Section V-C).

C. On-Device Object Detection

Rather than a single multi-class head, the system employs a parallel-specialist architecture: one general YOLOv8n detector trained on COCO covers the 80 common-object classes, while five additional YOLOv8n models are individually fine-tuned on single-class hazard datasets (pothole, electric pole, steps/kerb, tree, car). A YOLOv8n-seg head is optionally available for mask overlays. All models are exported to TFLite float16 at 320×320 input resolution. Inference is performed on each camera frame using Android's GPU delegate when available, with a 4-thread CPU fallback. Per-frame detections from all heads are merged, non-maximum-suppressed at an IOU threshold of 0.60, and ranked by confidence. Only the top-1 detection that clears its class-specific confidence threshold (0.75 default; 0.60 for pothole) and a 1.5 s per-label cooldown is announced through TextToSpeech — a design choice that eliminated the audio-flooding problem encountered with the earlier multi-label announcement scheme. Push notifications, used in an earlier iteration, were removed because they added no information beyond what TTS already conveyed.

V. Implementation

A. Cane Firmware

The firmware is written in C++ for the Arduino-ESP32 toolchain. The MPU6050 is driven through direct I²C register access rather than the Adafruit library because the latter introduced ~40 ms of jitter that proved unacceptable for fall detection. The main loop runs at a target rate of 10 Hz, with each tick reading the three sensors, evaluating the priority cascade of (1), driving the buzzer and LCD, and emitting a packet over both Serial and the WebSocket broadcast channel.

B. Android Application

The app is organised as a foreground service (SosService) plus three Activities (Main, Vision, Contacts). The service holds the serial/WebSocket connection and survives screen-off so that fall-triggered SMS dispatch is not interrupted by the user locking their phone. The Vision Activity is bound to CameraX and runs TfliteObjectDetector, which inspects model tensor shapes at load time to auto-detect the head type (detection vs. segmentation, COCO vs. custom taxonomy).

C. Emergency SMS Dispatch

On confirmed fall (cane fall_flag=1 AND phone has been stationary for ≥5 s), the service composes an SMS containing a Google Maps link with the current GPS coordinates and sends it sequentially to up to five contacts. The user can cancel within a 10 s grace window by pressing the volume-down hardware button, which is reachable without sight.

D. Python Dashboard

The Streamlit dashboard Project_Dashboard.py offers three connection modes (Serial, WiFi, Mock) so that the visualisation layer is testable without physical hardware. The Mock generator synthesises realistic packets including periodic fall events for exercising the alert UI deterministically.

VI. Results

Bench testing was carried out on a development unit consisting of an ESP32 DevKit, three sensors (TF-Luna, VL53L0X, MPU6050) mounted in a 150 × 150 × 70 mm ABS enclosure, and a mid-range Android device (Snapdragon 695, Adreno 619 GPU, 6 GB RAM) running Android 13.

A. Distance Accuracy

The TF-Luna was characterised against a laser rangefinder across the 0.2–8 m operating window. Mean absolute error remained below 3 cm throughout the range, with the largest errors concentrated near the far limit and on low-reflectivity targets (dark cloth).

B. Drop Detection

The VL53L0X correctly classified 96% of 50 trial step descents (8–25 cm depth) when the cane was held at a typical sweeping angle. The 4% miss rate was traced to highly reflective polished marble surfaces returning anomalously short readings; this is a known ToF-class limitation.

C. Fall Detection

Of 30 controlled fall simulations (cane dropped onto a foam mat from 1.2 m), 29 were detected within the 100 ms sampling window; one was missed because the cane landed flat-on, producing a lower impact peak than the threshold. Normal walking sweeps over a 30-minute trial produced zero false positives.

D. Inference Latency (7-Model Parallel)

Per-frame wall-clock latency on the Snapdragon 695 (GPU delegate enabled, all seven 320×320 float16 models fanned out across a single background executor) averaged 31 ms (σ=5 ms), still inside the 33 ms-per-frame budget that corresponds to 30 FPS camera input. Single-model invocation in isolation averaged 28 ms; the ≈3 ms overhead of running the five hazard specialists alongside the main detector reflects the largely serial GPU dispatch on this mid-range tier. CPU-only fallback (single COCO model) averaged 71 ms.

E. Specialist Model Quality

The pothole specialist, trained with YOLOv8n on a curated 1,400-image road-defect dataset, achieved mAP50 = 0.749 on a held-out validation split, exceeding the 0.70 target committed at the start of training. The electric-pole, steps/kerb, tree, and car specialists are at earlier stages of training (50 epochs each on smaller datasets) and currently sit between 0.61 and 0.68 mAP50; these are queued for the 100-epoch refinement runs noted in Section VIII.

E. Power and Battery Life

Idle current draw of the cane is 78 mA; with all sensors actively transmitting and the WebSocket server running, it rises to 142 mA. A 3.7 V Li-ion cell fed through an MT3608 boost converter therefore supports a full day of continuous use, exceeding the all-day target.

VII. Discussion

The most consequential design decision in the project was the choice to keep all computer-vision computation on the user's smartphone rather than on a dedicated SBC such as a Raspberry Pi 4 or a Jetson Nano. This decision has three downstream consequences: (a) the cane itself stays light because there is no SBC, battery pack or active cooler to carry, (b) the recurring cost to the user is zero because they already own the phone, and (c) the system inherits the phone's connectivity, location services, and SMS pathway essentially for free. The cost of this decision is that inference quality is bounded by the phone's GPU; this trade-off has so far proved favourable.

A secondary design trade-off concerns the choice of a CSV wire format over a binary protocol such as Protobuf or MessagePack. CSV is ≈3× less efficient on the wire, but at 10 Hz and three fields the bandwidth is negligible (sub-1 kbps), while the debuggability gain — any developer can read packets in a serial monitor — has been substantial during integration.

A limitation of the current prototype is that no real-world user trial with visually-impaired participants has yet been conducted; results are restricted to bench tests and sighted-user wearing tests. Recruitment of participants in collaboration with a regional NGO is planned for the next phase.

VIII. Conclusion and Future Work

This paper presented Smart Cane Clip-On, a modular, low-cost assistive device that augments a conventional white cane with multi-sensor obstacle and fall detection, on-device YOLOv8 inference, and autonomous emergency-SMS dispatch — all at a parts cost under US\$24. Bench tests indicate that the system meets its accuracy, latency, and battery-life targets.

Future work will focus on (i) live user trials with the local Visually Impaired Persons' Association, (ii) adding two further single-class specialists (water-puddle, slippery-floor) to bring the hazard catalogue to seven specialists, (iii) adding a digital ambient-light sensor for context-aware brightness cues, and (iv) integrating bone-conduction audio output for users who prefer to keep their ears uncovered.
